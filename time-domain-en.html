Research Direction

Time-Domain Astronomy / Data Processing

Time-domain astronomy studies a changing Universe: stellar explosions, black holes disrupting stars, gamma-ray bursts, and electromagnetic counterparts to gravitational-wave events.

Definition

What Is Time-Domain Astronomy?

Instead of treating the sky as a static deep image, time-domain astronomy asks how the sky changes. It studies periodic variables, irregular variables, and transients, asking where the variability comes from, how long it lasts, and what powers it.

Data processing is central to the field: image calibration, difference imaging, candidate filtering, machine-learning classification, light-curve modeling, and multiwavelength cross-matching turn raw images into scientifically useful alerts.

Surveys

Major Time-Domain Survey Projects

GOTTA

Global Open Transient Telescope Array

Led by the National Astronomical Observatories, CAS, GOTTA is a global open telescope array for fast transient discovery and coordinated follow-up.

LSST

Legacy Survey of Space and Time

Vera C. Rubin Observatory's LSST will produce a high-throughput alert stream through a long-term wide-field survey.

WFST

Wide Field Survey Telescope

WFST is a wide-field optical survey facility supporting transient searches, Solar System science, Galactic structure, and galaxy cosmology.

Science Cases

Common Science Targets

Supernovae

Supernovae arise from core collapse in massive stars or thermonuclear runaway in white dwarfs. Early light curves, colors, and spectra reveal explosion physics; Type Ia events also anchor cosmic distance measurements.

Tidal Disruption Events

When a star passes close to a supermassive black hole, tides can disrupt it and create a transient accretion flow. UV, optical, and X-ray light curves constrain black hole mass and reprocessing physics.

Gamma-Ray Bursts

Gamma-ray bursts are relativistic jet events from massive-star collapse or compact-object mergers. Rapid localization and afterglow monitoring connect prompt high-energy emission to the circumburst environment.

Gravitational-Wave Events

Binary neutron-star and neutron-star black-hole mergers can produce gravitational waves, kilonovae, short GRBs, and radio afterglows. Optical surveys must rapidly search large localization regions.
