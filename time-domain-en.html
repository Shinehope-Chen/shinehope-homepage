Definition

What Is Time-Domain Astronomy?

Instead of treating the sky as a static deep image, time-domain astronomy asks how the sky changes. It studies periodic variables, irregular variables, and transients, asking where the variability comes from, how long it lasts, and what powers it.

Data processing is central to the field: image calibration, difference imaging, candidate filtering, machine-learning classification, light-curve modeling, and multiwavelength cross-matching turn raw images into scientifically useful alerts.

Surveys

Major Time-Domain Survey Projects

GOTTA

Global Open Transient Telescope Array

Led by the National Astronomical Observatories, CAS, GOTTA is a global open telescope array for fast transient discovery and coordinated follow-up.

LSST

Legacy Survey of Space and Time

Vera C. Rubin Observatory's LSST will produce a high-throughput alert stream through a long-term wide-field survey.

WFST

Wide Field Survey Telescope

WFST is a wide-field optical survey facility supporting transient searches, Solar System science, Galactic structure, and galaxy cosmology.

Science Cases

Common Science Targets

Supernovae

Supernovae arise from core collapse in massive stars or thermonuclear runaway in white dwarfs. Time-domain surveys use early light curves, color evolution, and spectra to follow explosion physics; Type Ia supernovae also anchor cosmic distance measurements.

Image Source Review: Filippenko 1997

Tidal Disruption Events

When a star passes close to a supermassive black hole, tidal forces can tear it apart and form an accretion flow. UV, optical, and X-ray variability constrains black hole mass, disk formation, and reprocessed radiation.

Image Source Review: Gezari 2021

Gamma-Ray Bursts

Gamma-ray bursts are extreme relativistic-jet events that may follow massive-star collapse or compact-binary mergers. Rapid localization, afterglow light curves, and multiwavelength spectra probe jet energy and the surrounding medium.

Image Source Review: Piran 2004

Gravitational-Wave Events

Binary-neutron-star or neutron-star-black-hole mergers produce gravitational waves and may also create kilonovae, short gamma-ray bursts, and radio afterglows. Optical surveys must quickly filter candidates across large localization regions.

Image Source Review: Metzger 2017
